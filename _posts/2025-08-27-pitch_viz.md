My first foray into computational physics was a project for an undergread course where I simulated the projectile motion of a spinning baseball. Countless code iterations, two physics degrees, and three MLB pitch-tracking camera system updates later, that project has led to the [pitch visualizer](/pitch-graphic/) hosted on my website.

My program draws on the collection a papers by [Professor Alan Nathan](https://baseball.physics.illinois.edu/) of the University of Illinois. The general concept is to recover a pitch trajectory from the kinematic information provided at a single point in the path of an MLB pitch, accesible via the pitch-tracking data made available by the MLB.  

## Version 1
In my first iteration, I followed the protocol laid out in a [paper Nathan's posted on his website in 2015](https://baseball.physics.illinois.edu/trackman/spinaxis.pdf), and subsequently updated in 2018 and 2020. The approach is as follows.

MLB's pitch-tracking system (originally PITCHf/x, then Statcast using Trackman, and now Statcast using Hawkeye) provides nine + two key parameters that can be used describe all the forces[^1] acting on a spinning baseball at a single point[^2] in its trajectory:
* The first nine parameters, which Nathan coins 9P, are the three components of the **position**, **velocity**, and **acceleration** of the pitch when it is 50 feet from home plate. A small caveat: the velocity is actually given at the release point of the pitch, typically ~55 feet from homeplate (but this depends on a pitcher's "extension").
* The final two parameters are the **spin rate** and **spin-axis** of the baseball, which are assumed to be constant throughout the ~0.4 seconds the pitch is in flight[^3]. The full 3D spin-axis is not provided, but rather its projection in the $$x-z$$ plane (see coordinate system above) as a single angle $$\phi$$, the "spin direction."

Nathan decomposes the provided acceleration into three contributions, gravity, drag, and lift:

$$
\vec{a} = \vec{a}_G + \vec{a}_D + \vec{a}_L \tag{1}
$$

Gravity always points downwards ($$-\hat{z}$$), drag always points in the opposite direction of the velocity ($$-\hat{v}$$), and the lift points perpendicular to both the spin-axis and the velocity ($$\hat{\omega} \times \hat{v}$$). We don't have the full spin-axis, but Nathan does something clever to reconstruct it. He first subtracts the known contribution of gravity from the total acceleration

$$
\vec{a}^* = \vec{a} - \vec{a}_G = \vec{a}_D + \vec{a}_L  \tag{2}
$$

and then projects this onto the direction of velocity (actually the average velocity over the flight, to be consistent with the averaged 9P provided by Statcast). Since the acceleration due to lift is perpendicular to the velocity, this projection will isolate the contribution from the drag:

$$
\vec{a}_D = -\left(\vec{a}^* \cdot \langle \hat{v}\rangle\right)\langle \hat{v}\rangle  \tag{3}
$$

Following the same idea, he isolates the lift acceleration by subtracting $$\vec{a}_D$$ from  $$\vec{a}^*$$
 
$$
\vec{a}_L = \vec{a}^* - \vec{a}_D  \tag{4}
$$

Since the direction of $$\vec{a}_L$$ depends on the full 3D spin-axis and the velocity (which is known), $$\hat{\omega}$$ can be extracted with some simple algebra. In a similar vein, given the acceleration due to drag and lift, one can also extract the drag anf lift *coefficients*, $$C_D$$ and $$C_L$$. This gives everything needed to calculate the force on the baseball at any point in its trajectory, which can in turn be used to simulate its motion,

$$
\vec{F} = m\vec{a}_G - K C_D |\vec{v}|^2 \hat{v} + K C_L |\vec{v}|^2 \frac{\hat{\omega} \times \hat{v}} {|\hat{\omega} \times \hat{v}|},  \tag{5}
$$

where the coefficient $$K$$ depends on the air density and the shape/size of the baseball. There are some subtleties that I glossed over (such as factoring in *gyrospin* into the reconstruction of $$\hat{\omega}$$), so I implore the curious reader to refer to the [full paper](https://baseball.physics.illinois.edu/trackman/spinaxis.pdf) for further detail (where it's all laid out quite nicely).


## Version 2

With the implementation of the Hawkeye camera system in 2020, we were finally able to gauge the accuracy of this method of inferring the spin-axis. Why would this method be innacurate? Well in this calculation, we assume that all the non-gravity and non-drag motion (i.e. spin-induced motion) is due to the Magnus effect. But this is only true for a spinning object with a *smooth* surface. If instead, the spinning projectile has some assymetrical surface roughness, there will be turbulance effects that induce *non-Magnus* motion, see videos below.

### Seam-Shifted Wake (SSW)

The seams on a baseball provide exactly the type of asymmetric roughness needed for this type of movement, and as such, this effect has been colloquialized as **seam-shifted wake (SSW)**. A now well understood and emphasized effect by baseball analysts and pitching coaches, this concept was first explored in depth by by Nathan, along with Professor Barton Smith and Harry Pavlidis, in their 2020 paper [Not Just About Magnus Anymore](https://www.baseballprospectus.com/news/article/62912/not-just-about-magnus-anymore/). Their smoking gun result, strongly suggesting that there was something other than Magnus spin-induced motion at play, was a series of plots comparing the inferred and Hawkeye observed spin-direction for various pitch types and pitcher handedness. Any disagreement between these measures reflected an improper attribution of SSW force to the Magnus force, introducing a systematic error in the inferred spin-axis calculation. This result is reproduced below.

The inferred and observed spin axes align well for four-seam fastballs, and to some degree, for curveballs too. For sinkers and changeups, however, we instead see a systematic disagreement between the two spin directions. This all comes down to seam orientation.


The SSW effect is caused by oncoming air "sticking" to the seams, creating a pressure differential on opposite sides of the spinning ball. But this effect only creates lift if the seams are orientation such that the SSW forces do not cancel out during a rotation --- as is the case for a four-seamer. For a four-seam grip, as shown in the right, a wake will form on both the top and bottom of ball twice during per rotation, and the induced motion will cancel out. For the two-seamer/sinker grip shown below it, the oncoming air will see a much more consistent ring of seams on the left hand side of the spinning baseball, compared to the relatively smoother center and right sides (which is most notbale when the baseball is [spinning very fast](/assets/pitch_traj/fs_fast.mp4)). This assymmetry is what is necessary for SSW to induce significant movement, and achieved by introducing some gyrospin into a pitch thrown with a two/one-seam grip.

  
### Modeling SSW
While the mechanism behind SSW is now well understood, modeling SSW forces is not a nearly as straightforward. The key piece of information that is missing in the public data is seam orientation --- with this detail, a robust SSW model could likely be developed. To my knowledge, Hawkeye does measure this, but only provides access to teams and backend users.

But this does not mean that we have to ignore SSW completely in our trajectory modeling. Borrowing from the physics toolbox, we can developed an *effective* model of SSW, utlizing some basics from fluid dynamics. As Nathan breaks down in his [2021 paper](https://baseball.physics.illinois.edu/HawkeyeSpinAnalysis-MLB-Part%20II-v2-public.pdf), we can further breakdown $$\vec{a}_L$$ into perpendicular Magnus and side components:

$$
\vec{a}_L = \vec{a}_M + \vec{a}_S  \tag{6}
$$

With access to the full 3D spin axis, $$\hat{\omega}$$, we can calculate these two components exactly,


$$
\begin{aligned}
\vec{a}_M &= |\vec{a}_L| \cos(\theta_{L-M}) \left[\frac{\hat{\omega} \times \hat{v}}{|\hat{\omega} \times \hat{v}|}\right] = |\vec{a}_L| \cos(\theta_{L-M}) \left[\frac{\hat{\omega} \times \hat{v}}{\eta}\right]\\\\
\vec{a}_S &= |\vec{a}_L| \sin(\theta_{L-M}) \left[\frac{\hat{\omega} \times \left(\hat{\omega} \times \hat{v}\right)}{|\hat{\omega} \times \left(\hat{\omega} \times \hat{v}\right)|}\right] = |\vec{a}_L| \sin(\theta_{L-M}) \left[\frac{\hat{\omega} \times \left(\hat{\omega} \times \hat{v}\right)}{\eta}\right].
\end{aligned}
$$

Both $$\hat{a}_L$$, given by Eq. (4), and $$\hat{a}_M$$, given by $$\hat{\omega} \times \hat{v}$$,  are perpendicular to the velocity, so they will lie in the same plane. If SSW effects are negligible, as for four-seam fastballs, these vectors will coincide. Otherwise, they will be seperated by an angle $$\theta_{L-M}$$, which encodes the distribution of motion lift between Magnus and SSW (see figure to the right). We now also introduce the *spin efficiency* $$\eta$$, which measures how perpendicular $$\hat{\omega}$$ is to $$\hat{v}$$ (averaged over the pitch trajectory) --- this encodes the amount of gyrospin a pitch has.
