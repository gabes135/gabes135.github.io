My first foray into computational physics was a project for a course in undegrad where I simulated the projectile motion of a spinning baseball. Countless code iteractions, two physics degrees, and three MLB pitch-tracking camera system updates later, that project has led to the [pitch visualizer](/pitch-graphic/) hosted on my website.

My program draws on the collection a papers by [Professor Alan Nathan](https://baseball.physics.illinois.edu/) of the University of Illinois. The general concept is to recover a pitch trajectory from the kinematic information of a single point in the motion of an MLB pitch, accesible via pitch-tracking data made available by the MLB.  

## Version 1
In my first iteration, I followed the protocol laid out in a [paper Nathan's posted on his website in 2015](https://baseball.physics.illinois.edu/trackman/spinaxis.pdf), and subsequently updated in 2018 and 2020. The approach is as follows:

MLB's pitch-tracking system (originally PITCHf/x, then Statcast using Trackman, and now Statcast using Hawkeye) provides nine + two key parameters that can be used describe all the forces[^1] acting on a spin baseball at a single point[^2] in its trajectory 
* The first nine parameters, which Nathan coins 9P, are the three components of the **position**, **velocity**, and **acceleration** of the pitch when it is 50 feet from home plate. A small caveat, the velocity is actually given at the release point of the pitch, typically ~55 feet from homeplate (but this depends on a pitcher's "extension").
* The second two parameters are the **spin rate** and **spin-axis** of the baseball, which are assumed to be constant throughout the ~0.4 seconds the pitch is in flight[^3]. The full 3D spin-axis is not provided, but rather its projection in the $$x-z$$ plane (see coordinate system above) as a single angle $$\phi$$, the "spin direction."

The provided acceleration is decomposed into three contributions, gravity, drag, and lift:

$$
\vec{a} = \vec{a}_G + \vec{a}_D + \vec{a}_L
$$

Gravity always points downwards ($$-\hat{z}$$), drag always points in the opposite direction of the velocity ($$-\hat{v}$$), and the lift points perpendicular to both the spin-axis and the velocity ($$\hat{\omega} \times \hat{v}$$). We don't have the full spin-axis, but Nathan does something clever to reconstruct it. He first subtracts the known contribution of gravity from the total acceleration

$$
\vec{a}^* = \vec{a} - \vec{a}_G = \vec{a}_D + \vec{a}_L
$$

and then projects this onto the direction of velocity (the average velocity over the flight, to be consistent with the averaged 9P provided by Statcast). Since the acceleration due to lift is perpendicular to the velocity, this projection will isolate the contribution from the drag:

$$
\vec{a}_D = -\left(\vec{a}^* \cdot \langle \hat{v}\rangle\right)\langle \hat{v}\rangle
$$

Following the same idea, he isolates the lift acceleration by subtracting $$\vec{a}_D$$ from  $$\vec{a}^*$$

$$
\vec{a}_L = \vec{a}^* - \vec{a}_D
$$

Since the direction of $$\vec{a}_L$$ depends on the full 3D spin-axis and the velocity (which is known), $$\hat{omega}$$ can be extracted with some simple algebra. 

There are some subtleties that I glossed over, but I implore the reader to refer to the [full paper](https://baseball.physics.illinois.edu/trackman/spinaxis.pdf) for further detail (where it's all laid out quite nicely).
